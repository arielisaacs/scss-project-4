Most women do not choose a life of prostitution; for most, it is a last resort to survive, find shelter, and keep their children off the street. Most were coerced in their adolescence, and many struggle to break free from violent pimps. Many are held back by drug addiction, something they came to depend upon to cope with unfathomable hardship. Most have been penalized by the authorities and all struggle to overcome the societal obstacles that stand in their way of an endurable life. As such, prostitution should not be considered the ill itself, but an inevitable consequence of a broken system.
Until all children, regardless of their birth-given circumstances, gain access to a safe place to live, a quality education, health care, and a nutritious diet, we will never be able to prevent the most vulnerable girls and young women from turning to prostitution as a means to attain them, nor from escaping it once started. Furthermore, it should be the aim of all communities to provide their children with stable parental guidance and a visible path to financial security within the boundaries of law. These are not to be considered unobtainable ideals, but the defining qualities of a developed nation.
Despite a running time of less than 28 minutes, Hold Me Down would take nearly three years to complete due to the complex nature of the production. Filmed on location in the South Bronx, the narrative spans from the Mott Haven Projects to the underground locales where the events depicted actually occur. In the words of Carole Dean: (President of the Roy W. Dean Film Grants) “There has never been a film like this before; It is really in a category of its own.”
In the U.N. Declaration of Human Rights, article 25, it reads:
“Everyone has the right to a standard of living adequate for the health and well-being of himself and of his family, including food, clothing, housing and medical care and necessary social services, and the right to security in the event of unemployment, sickness, disability, widowhood, old age or other lack of livelihood in circumstances beyond his control.” Furthermore, “all children, whether born in or out of wedlock, shall enjoy the same social protection.”
According to recent studies by the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development, the U.S. currently has the second highest level of income inequality in the developed world, second only to Chile; and the second highest level of income heritability, second only to the United Kingdom; which means that there is almost no other country in the developed world where the disparity between rich and poor is greater, and the likelihood of a person making it from one to the other is worse.
Still, Americans reportedly have more faith in their own social mobility than any other national in the developed world. With such a discrepancy between the reality and people’s perception thereof, we feel that there is a real need to start a conversation.
